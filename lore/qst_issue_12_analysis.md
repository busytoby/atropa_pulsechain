# QST Issue #12 Technical Analysis: Hot-Wire Ammeter Thermal Dynamics

A physical modeling study of the high-frequency current measurement methods described by Stuart Ballantine in the January 1917 issue of *QST* ("The Measurement of High-Frequency Currents").

---

## 1. Historical & Technical Context

In the January 1917 issue of *QST* (Volume 2, Number 2), Charles Stuart Ballantine published **"The Measurement of High-Frequency Currents"**. The paper investigated the sources of measurement errors when using **hot-wire ammeters** to read antenna currents under radio-frequency (RF) excitation. 

Amateurs of the era used hot-wire ammeters calibrated with direct current (DC) to tune their transmitter tanks. Ballantine showed that high-frequency currents cause uneven current distribution along the heating wire (due to skin effect and shunt capacity), rendering standard DC calibrations inaccurate.

## 2. Mathematical Hot-Wire Ammeter Model

Under the zero-loss algebraic invariant ($\Phi = 0$), the physical indicator maps the thermal energy generated by Joule heating directly to linear expansion of the metal wire.

The heating wire is characterized by mass ($m$), specific heat capacity ($c_p$), nominal resistance ($R_0$), and temperature coefficient of resistance ($\alpha$).

### Joule Heating & Heat Balance Equation:

The rate of temperature change ($dT/dt$) of the platinum-iridium wire is governed by input Joule power and radiative/convective heat dissipation:

$$m \cdot c_p \frac{dT(t)}{dt} = i_{\text{rf}}^2(t) \cdot R(T) - k_{\text{loss}} \cdot (T(t) - T_{\text{ambient}})$$

Where the temperature-dependent resistance is:

$$R(T) = R_0 \cdot (1.0 + \alpha \cdot (T(t) - T_{\text{ambient}}))$$

### Mechanical Deflection:
The pointer needle deflection ($\theta(t)$) is proportional to the linear expansion of the wire ($\Delta L(t)$), which depends on the coefficient of thermal expansion ($\lambda_{\text{exp}}$):

$$\Delta L(t) = L_0 \cdot \lambda_{\text{exp}} \cdot (T(t) - T_{\text{ambient}})$$

$$\theta(t) = K_{\text{pointer}} \cdot \Delta L(t)$$

Because the pointer system has mechanical mass ($M_{\text{needle}}$) and spring damping ($B_{\text{needle}}$), it behaves as a second-order low-pass filter:

$$M_{\text{needle}} \frac{d^2 \theta(t)}{dt^2} + B_{\text{needle}} \frac{d\theta(t)}{dt} + K_{\text{spring}} \theta(t) = K_{\text{torque}} \cdot \Delta L(t)$$
