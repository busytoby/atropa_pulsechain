# The Auditory Telemetry of Hollerith Tone Wheels

In the early design specs of the **Auncient** Dysnomia VM and the WinchesterMQ physical interface layers, character input systems were not static electronic arrays. Instead, they relied on electromechanical and optical resonance chambers driven by rotating **Hollerith Tone Wheels**. These rotating cylinders, wrapped in punched cards, functioned as mechanical wavetable synthesizers and matched-filter transceivers.

## 1. The Harmonic Grid

An 80-column by 12-row Hollerith card wrapped onto a drum represents 12 concentric frequency tracks. As the drum rotates under a common sensor bridge, each column outputs a parallel bit pattern. 
* By matching the physical hole positions to specific harmonic ratios, a single card generates structured chord configurations.
* Standard character encodings are mapped to distinct acoustic profiles. For instance, the **Auncient** systems translated ASCII and EBCDIC character inputs directly into audio frequency shifts (FSK) to transmit data across low-bandwidth analog links.

---

## 2. Universal Transcoding & Preemption Gating

Because the tone wheels operate on purely physical and mechanical principles, the same rotating structure functions as both the modulator (encoder) and demodulator (decoder):

### The Encoding Phase
Digital inputs feed high-speed solenoid punchers that mark the rotating card surface, or gate electrical contacts passing over pre-punched tracks. The rotation generates corresponding audio patterns:
* **Morse Code:** Gated via serial envelope tracks where dots occupy single columns and dashes span three.
* **Baudot (ITA2) & Murray Codes:** Modulated by switching between Letters (LTRS) and Figures (FIGS) zone tracks to shift the base carrier frequency.
* **FIELDATA & BCDIC:** Driven through 6-bit parallel sensors directly feeding an R-2R ladder to output stepped voltage patterns.
* **Radix-50:** Compressed 3-character words are decoded by multiplying base radices mechanically to scale phase angles in the phase-locked loops.

### The Decoding Phase
Incoming audio signals are routed into the SAGE phase-locked loop (PLL) fabric. The PLL tracks the input frequency and compares its phase error against the reference signals generated by the rotating wheel. When the phase error drops to zero, the system identifies the matching track, decodes the character, and writes it directly to the WinchesterMQ SCSI registers.
